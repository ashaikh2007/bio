Animals

You body has many systems that work together to keep it functioning. These include the digestive system, respiratory system, and the circulatory system


The Digestive System

The digestive system is responsible for breaking down food (macromolecules), turning it into usable substances (monomers), and releasing waste product. It has 4 phases
  • Ingestion - taking in food
  • Digestion - Breaking down food using various digestive enzymes
  • Absorbtion - Transporting digested food to bloodstream
  • Elimination - Excretion of waste

    • Macromolecules

    All food we eat is made from either carbs, proteins, or fats. Each one is broken down using specific enzymes so our bodies can utilize them. Here are all the macromolecules and their info

    Carbs

  • Chemical composition is CHO

  • Exists as both complex sugars (glycogen, starch, cellulose) and as simple sugars (glucose, fructose, galactose)

  • Breaks down into monomer glucose

  • Used as energy

    • Proteins

  • Chemical composition is CHNO

  • Breaks down into monomer animo acids

  • Used to build and repair cells + used as enzymes

  • Body uses 9 essential amino acids to make its own proteins

    • Fats

  • Chemical composition is CHO

  • Breaks down into glycerol + 3 fatty acids

  • Used for energy, insulation, cushioning, storage, and other things

    • Parts and functions of the digestive system

    The mouth
  • Made up of teeth, the tongue, and salivary glands
  • Teech mechanically break down food
  • Tongue turns foods into ball like shape and help send down pharynx
  • Salivary glands produce saliva, which chemically breaks down carbs into glucose using amylyse enzyme

    • The Pharynx
  • Passage after mouth that seperates air and food using epiglottis flap

    • The Esophagus
  • Transports food from mouth to stomach
  • Uses muscles to move food down in process called peristalsis

    • The Stomach
  • J shaped
  • Mechanically breaks down food through contracting
  • Chemically breaks down food using HCL and pepsin(digests proteins)
  • Digested food gets absorbed into bloodstream, undigested food is passed down into small intestine
  • Stomach intake and outlet controlled by esophageal and pyloric sphincter respectivly

    • The Small intestine
  • Contains small protrusions call villi and microvilli to increase surface area and aid in digestion
  • Can be seperated into 3 distinct areas
  • Duodenum - Mostly digestion. Enzymes bile(breaks down fat), peptidase(breaks down proteins), lipase(breaks down fats), amylase(breaks down cabs), and other digestive enzymes are released
  • Jejunum - Mix of disgestion and absorbtion of nutrients into blood
  • Ileum - Mostly absorbtion of nutrients into blood
  • Remaining undigested food sent to large intestine

    • Large intestine
  • Absorbs water from undigested food

    • Rectum
  • Excretes undigested food



    • The Respiratory System

    The respiratory system is responsible exchanging CO2 in blood for oxygen. Oxygen esessential for most life on Earth (some organisms use other gases)

    Parts and functions of the respiratory system

    The Nasal Cavity
  • Moistens air
  • Filters air using nose hair and mucus

    • Pharynx
  • Connects mouth and nose
  • Redirects air into larynx and esophagus using epiglottis

    • Larynx
  • Contains voice box

    • Trachea
  • Brings air into right and left bronchi (singlular bronchus)
  • Contains cillia, which are hair like projectuions that move dirt and debris out of airway
  • Contains goblet cells, which produce mucous that traps dirt and protects inner lining

    • Bronchi
  • Splits airway into bronchioles
  • Contains cillia, which are hair like projectuions that move dirt and debris out of airway
  • Contains goblet cells, which produce mucous that traps dirt and protects inner lining

    • Bronchioles
  • Smallest pathway
  • Connect to alveoli
  • Contains cillia, which are hair like projectuions that move dirt and debris out of airway
  • Contains goblet cells, which produce mucous that traps dirt and protects inner lining

    • Alveoli
  • Where gas exchange occurs (CO2 repalced with oxygen)
  • Capillaries line outside of alveoli
  • Since alveoli have thin moist walls, oxygen dissolves into moisture before diffusing across into blood in capillaries (low concentration of oxygen in blood causes it to be drawn in)
  • CO2 in blood diffuses into alveoli (low concentration of CO2 in alveoli causes it to be drawn in)
  • High surface area of combined 300 million alveoli make it effective at providing blood with oxygen

    • Diaphram
  • Contracts (moves down) to breathe in (lungs get expanded, so air rushes in)
  • Relaxes (moves up) to breathe out (lungs get contracted, so air rushes out)



    • The Circulatory System

    The circulatory system is responsible for delivering blood to tissue all around your body. Blood contains oxygen, as well as vital nutrients needed by your tissue.

    Different blood vessles around the body

    Arteries
  • Carries blood away from heart
  • Thickm, elastic walls since it handles high pressure
  • Splits into smalled arterioles

    • Arterioles
  • Blood flows through if needed in tissue
  • Splits into capillaries

    • Capillaries
  • Has non elastic, thin walls due to low pressure
  • Blood forced out into surrounding tissue due to pressure
  • Blood in tissues returns through osmosis (blood concentration low in capillaries)
  • Blood from capillaries flows into venules

    • Venules
  • Blood flows into veins

    • Veins
  • Thin, elastic walls due to relativly low pressure
  • Has valves and muscle tissure around to prevent backflow
  • Takes blood back to heart

    • Reoxygenating blood

    Each time you heart beats, it actually beats twice. The first time, it pumps blood through the heart (pulmonary circuit ). The second time, the oxygenated blood is pumped through the rest of the body (systemetic circuit). Once blood has went around the body, it become deoxygenated, and is filled with CO2. It needs to travel into the lungs to be re-oxygenated through the pulmonary circuit. The heart utilizes its 4 chambers to do this

    The pulmonary circuit

  • Deoxygenated blood flows into heart through vena cava into right atrium
  • Blood flows from right atrium to right ventricle
  • Blood is pumped from right ventricle through pulmonary artery
  • Blood flows from pulmonary artery into capillaries that line alveoli, and gas exchange occurs
  • Blood flows from capillaries in lungs to pulmonary vein
  • Blood flows from pulmonary vein into left atrium, and then into left ventricle
  • Blood is pumped from left ventricle through aorta
